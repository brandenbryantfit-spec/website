The Truth Most People Miss

Surgery was step one.
Not the solution.

"The surgery removed the crisis. The disc is still there. The level is still vulnerable. The muscles that were supposed to protect that disc — they're still weak, still deconditioned, still not doing their job."

Re-herniation rates exist for a reason. The structural environment that allowed the first herniation hasn't changed. Without deliberately rebuilding the posterior chain and deep core stability around that level, the disc remains exposed to the same forces that caused this in the first place.

⚠ What Most People Do Wrong
Most people jump immediately into back extensions, side bending, and rotational movements during the acute phase — and end up setting themselves back weeks or months. The spine needs to be stabilized before it can be loaded. Isometric work first. Movement patterns second. Loading third. There is a time and place for every exercise. The problem is never the exercise — it's the timing.

The Protocol

12 months. Four phases.
One outcome.

Every phase has entry criteria. You don't advance on a calendar — you advance when your body demonstrates readiness. This is what separates a real protocol from a generic program.

01
Weeks 2–6
Neural Calming & Movement Restoration
Goal — Reduce irritability. Protect the surgical site. Begin nerve recovery.
  • Diaphragmatic breathing — rebuilds intra-abdominal pressure control passively
  • Walking progression — 10 min to 45+ min. Most underrated post-surgical intervention
  • Supine heel slides and abdominal bracing — zero load, pure neuromuscular re-education
  • Sciatic nerve flossing — gentle neurodynamic mobilization to keep S1 pathway mobile during recovery
  • Seated calf raises — directly targets plantar flexion deficit from S1 involvement
  • Posture and positioning education — sitting tolerance critical for cockpit return
Milestone: Walking 30+ min continuously with no increase in leg symptoms
02
Weeks 6–12
Stability Foundation
Goal — Deep core endurance. McGill stability framework. Posterior chain reactivation.
  • McGill Big Three — Bird dog, modified curl up, side plank. Most evidence-backed post-surgical stability protocol globally
  • Dead bugs — anti-extension core stability under controlled load
  • Glute bridges — reintroduced when pain-free, progressing to single leg
  • Standing calf raise progression — monitoring left vs right symmetry as S1 nerve recovery indicator
  • Hip hinge motor pattern — bodyweight only, establishing movement mechanics before load
  • Breathing under load — intra-abdominal pressure management during all exercises
Milestone: All McGill Big Three performed with zero symptom provocation. Walking 45–60 min. Improving calf raise symmetry.
03
Months 3–5
Posterior Chain Rebuilding
Goal — Graded loading. Disc protection through range mastery. Structural rebuilding.
  • Back extension iso holds 2-leg — building posterior chain endurance without spinal flexion under load
  • Single leg iso hold progression — once 2-leg is fully mastered
  • Quarter rep introduction month 4 — only after significant iso hold improvement
  • 3-week increment progression: ¼ rep → ½ rep → ¾ rep → full rep
  • Pallof press — anti-rotation core stability under load
  • Suitcase carries, goblet squat, step ups — progressive functional loading
  • Hip flexor strengthening — psoas attaches to lumbar vertebrae, weakness creates instability
Milestone: Full range back extension pain-free and strong. No residual leg symptoms during loading.
04
Month 6+
Athletic Reconstruction
Goal — Work toward Crossfit and Hyrox capacity. Maximize long term resilience. Prevent recurrence.
  • Trap bar deadlift progression — 8–12 week build from light to working weight
  • Bulgarian split squat, farmers carries, heavy loaded step ups
  • Running reintroduction — walk/run intervals progressing to continuous running as tolerated
  • Assault bike and rowing — cardiovascular base rebuilt without axial compression
  • Hyrox-specific movement reintroduction — programmed and sequenced based on your response
  • Re-herniation prevention principles embedded permanently into training approach
Milestone: Maximum strength symmetry achieved. Neurological symptoms minimized. Best possible athletic capacity restored. Aviation medical assessment prepared for.
